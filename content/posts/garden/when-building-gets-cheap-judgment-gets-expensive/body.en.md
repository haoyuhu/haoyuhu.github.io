My current working assumption is simple: as the cost of implementation keeps dropping, the role of the engineer shifts upward.

What becomes scarce is not raw ability to produce software. It is the ability to **choose the right abstraction**, decide which problems are actually worth solving, and design systems where humans and AI can contribute together without creating chaos.

That shift blurs the old boundary between software engineering and product thinking. In many cases, the bottleneck will no longer be, “Can we build this?” It will be:

- **Should this exist at all?**
- **What is the smallest useful abstraction?**
- **Where should human judgment remain in the loop?**
- **How do we keep the system understandable as it grows?**

This is the part I keep coming back to: AI does not erase software structure. It **amplifies** it.

If a codebase is well-factored, explicit, and conceptually clean, AI can accelerate extension and iteration. If the structure is muddy, ownership is vague, and abstractions leak everywhere, AI will happily scale the mess. You do not get less complexity; you get complexity produced faster.

So the hard part of engineering increasingly looks like framing:

- defining boundaries
- naming concepts well
- preserving intent
- setting up feedback loops
- managing the social and technical complexity around the code

In that world, software is no longer just something we build. It becomes a medium for coordination between people, models, and future maintainers.

The practical takeaway: write and design as if both a human teammate and an AI agent will need to understand your system tomorrow. Because they probably will.
