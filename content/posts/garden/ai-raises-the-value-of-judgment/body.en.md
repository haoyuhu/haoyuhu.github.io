AI does not feel like a substitute for serious thinking. It feels more like a compression layer on the legible parts of thinking: retrieval, first-pass synthesis, drafting, translation, enumeration, and cleanup. As those operations get cheaper, the center of gravity shifts.

What becomes more valuable is not raw output, but **choosing what should exist**.

A useful mental model:
- **AI expands option space.** It can generate many plausible answers, structures, and directions.
- **Humans still define the objective function.** We choose the goal, the constraints, the tradeoffs, and what “good” actually means.
- **The bottleneck moves upstream.** Problem framing, taste, prioritization, and responsibility matter more, not less.

This is why I do not buy the simple “AI replaces high-IQ work” story. High-level work was never just about producing text, code, or analysis. It was about:
- **Judgment** — knowing which problem is worth solving and when not to optimize.
- **Creativity** — reframing the problem, crossing domains, seeing a non-obvious path.
- **Big-picture intuition** — holding second-order effects, context, timing, and human consequences in view.

Models can be impressive locally while still being shallow globally. They can answer a question without understanding why the question matters. They can optimize a task without sensing the system around it.

So the real shift is not replacement, but leverage. AI increases the amount one person can explore, test, and ship. But the more capability we hand to machines, the more expensive bad judgment becomes.

In that sense, AI is not shrinking human importance. It is clarifying where human importance actually lives.
