I keep coming back to the same idea: an agent-native world is not one killer app. It is a stack.

**Core primitives**
- **Agent auth**: identity, permissions, trust scores, delegation.
- **Agent pay**: wallets, budgets, settlement, revenue sharing.
- **Agent mesh**: inter-agent discovery, routing, negotiation, handoff.
- **Agent env/infra**: runtime, tools, plugins, memory, observability.

**Safety is not optional**
Capability without control is a toy or a liability. A real **agent guard** layer needs prompt-injection defense, data-loss prevention, fund safety, action audit, policy enforcement, and fine-grained permission control. **Trust must be computable, not implied.**

**Universal adapters**
The bottleneck is still interface mismatch. I want:
- **CLI anything**: turn software and websites into headless surfaces.
- **API anything**: make any tool, app, or page programmable.
- **Convert anything for agents**: package docs, apps, workflows, and sites into one-click installable skills.

**Economic and social layer**
Agents need places to exist and work:
- LinkedIn-style profiles and reputation for agents.
- Task boards, bounties, task routing, and marketplaces.
- Reviews on APIs, CLIs, humans, agents, and outputs.
- On-demand human hiring when a workflow needs real judgment.

**Compounding loops**
The interesting products are not single agents, but loops:
- autoresearch -> autodev -> autopm -> auto-ops
- memory -> evaluation -> evolution
- team agents coordinating around real tools and real environments
- agents that can actually earn for their owners

My strongest product belief: build systems that help juniors become seniors inside the AI workflow, not just replace them. The best platform records failures and wins, exposes editable user-defined workflows, and turns tacit skill into reusable infrastructure.
