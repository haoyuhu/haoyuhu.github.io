My current bias for AI tools is simple: use the model to expand the option space, not to play schoolteacher.

When an assistant gives me one polished answer and asks for approval, I become a reviewer. That mode is narrow, slightly passive, and oddly tiring. But when it gives me 3-5 genuinely different candidates, the interaction changes. I am no longer grading a machine. I am choosing, combining, and steering.

That matters because humans are often better at **selection** than raw generation. Faced with a blank page, we stall. Faced with a handful of plausible directions, we can quickly say:
- this one has the right energy
- this structure is cleaner
- this phrase is worth keeping
- combine A’s framing with C’s ending

In other words: turn creation into a multiple-choice problem for the human.

This is especially useful in writing, naming, interface copy, visual directions, and early product thinking. The model should produce diversity, not just paraphrases. Different tones, structures, levels of ambition, and risk profiles. Ideally each option makes a distinct bet.

A good system, then, does three things:
- generates multiple candidates by default
- makes the differences legible
- lets me merge, mutate, and request another round

I trust AI most when it helps me explore. I trust humans most when a choice needs taste, context, or responsibility. So the split of labor feels obvious: let intelligence do the branching; let the person do the choosing.
