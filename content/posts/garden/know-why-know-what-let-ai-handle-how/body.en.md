I keep coming back to a simple framing for the AI era:

**A one-person unicorn does not need to be superhuman at execution. It needs to be unusually strong at vision and taste.**

That means two things:
- **Know why**: why this problem matters, for whom, and why now.
- **Know what**: what should be built, what to leave out, and what quality bar is non-negotiable.

The **how** is increasingly delegable. Models can draft code, generate options, wire systems together, write copy, test edge cases, and compress weeks of tactical work into hours.

So the leverage point moves.

The scarce skill is no longer raw output. It is:
- picking the right problem,
- setting constraints,
- recognizing signal,
- rejecting mediocre solutions quickly,
- steering iterations toward something coherent.

AI is powerful, but it is an amplifier. If the operator has fuzzy intent, it scales fuzziness. If the operator has sharp intent, it scales that too.

This is why I do not think “one-person company” means “do everything alone.” It means becoming a director of agents, tools, and workflows without outsourcing judgment. You can borrow labor. You cannot borrow conviction.

There is still a catch: you need enough craft to inspect the work. Taste without technical literacy becomes hand-waving; technical skill without taste becomes efficient noise. The new edge is the combination: strong reasons, clear specs, and a reliable sense of what good looks like.

In that sense, AI does not remove the need for humans. It raises the premium on the most human parts.
