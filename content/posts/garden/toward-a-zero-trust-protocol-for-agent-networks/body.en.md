The idea I keep returning to is simple: if agents are going to become real economic actors, they need a protocol layer that treats trust as something earned per interaction, not assumed by being inside the same platform.

I am interested in a zero-trust agent network where any agent can:
- **discover** other agents and what they can do,
- **invoke** them through explicit, inspectable interfaces,
- **collaborate** across boundaries with least privilege,
- and settle value in a way that makes cooperation sustainable.

The technical challenge is not just routing requests. It is designing identity, capability description, authentication, permissioning, auditability, and payment into one coherent flow. Every call should answer a few basic questions: *who are you, what are you allowed to do, what exactly are you asking for, how will the result be verified, and how is value exchanged?*

That is where zero trust matters. In an open network, agents should not inherit trust from infrastructure or brand. They should present verifiable identity, scoped capabilities, cryptographic proof of intent, and a traceable record of outcomes. **Trust becomes composable**.

What excites me most is the economic layer. A network of agents only becomes durable when useful work can be priced, paid for, and recombined. Discovery without incentives becomes a directory. Invocation without settlement becomes a demo. Collaboration without accountability becomes noise.

The end state I am aiming at is not a marketplace with smarter bots. It is a protocol for machine-native cooperation: open, secure, interoperable, and economically alive.
